# $100M Offers - Key Quotes
Source: Alex Hormozi, "$100M Offers"

---

## On Supply, Demand & Desire

> "Fundamentally, all marketing exists to influence the supply and demand curve. We artificially increase the demand for our products and services through some sort of persuasive communication."
-- Chapter 11

> "Desire is a contract you make with yourself to be unhappy until you get what you want."
-- Naval Ravikant, quoted in Chapter 11

> "The longer you delay the ask, the bigger the ask you can make. 'The longer the runway, the bigger the plane that can take off.'"
-- Chapter 11 (Hormozi Law)

> "We must endeavor to keep our supply (and satisfaction of desire) under the demand that we are able to generate. This maximizes profits and keeps desire ravenous in our customer base."
-- Chapter 11

> "When demand increases, cut supply."
-- George (jewelry businessman), Chapter 11

> "The person who needs the exchange less always has the upper hand."
-- Chapter 12

## On Scarcity

> "Fear of loss is stronger than desire for gain."
-- Chapter 12

> "When there's a fixed supply or quantity of products or services that are available for purchase it creates 'scarcity' or a 'fear of missing out.'"
-- Chapter 12

> "Important point: to properly utilize this method you should always sell out."
-- Chapter 12

> "When using this tactic, you must also let everyone know that you sold out. That is part of what makes it work so well."
-- Chapter 12

> "The easiest scarcity strategy is honesty."
-- Chapter 12

> "Scarcity also implies within it, social proof."
-- Chapter 12

## On Urgency

> "Scarcity is a function of quantity. Urgency is a function of time."
-- Chapter 13

> "Deadlines. Drive. Decisions."
-- Chapter 13

> "The biggest sales on a week long campaign or launch happen in the last 4 hours of the last day (up to 50-60%). That means that the last 3% of the time allotted creates 50-60% of the sales...that's completely illogical, but also unmistakably human."
-- Chapter 13

> "The actual promotion may be the same, but naming it something different 'by season' gives you a 'real' differentiator that gives you a start and a finish."
-- Chapter 13

> "Never raise your prices without letting people know."
-- Chapter 13

## On Bonuses

> "A single offer is less valuable than the same offer broken into its component parts and stacked as bonuses."
-- Chapter 14

> "Whenever trying to close a deal, never discount the main offer. It teaches your customers that your prices are negotiable (which is terrible). Adding bonuses to increase value to close the deal is far superior to cutting prices."
-- Chapter 14

> "The reason this works is we are increasing the prospect's price-to-value discrepancy by increasing the value delivered instead of cutting the price."
-- Chapter 14

> "People have a hard time rejecting reciprocity, so adding a bonus to accommodate, then another, then another, and people will feel almost obligated to buy from you."
-- Chapter 14

> "Tools & checklists are better than additional trainings (as the effort & time are lower with the former, so the value is higher)."
-- Chapter 14

> "The value of the bonuses should eclipse the value of the core offer."
-- Chapter 14

## On Guarantees

> "The single greatest objection for any product or service being sold is...risk."
-- Chapter 15

> "What makes a guarantee have power is a conditional statement: If you do not get X result in Y time period, we will Z."
-- Chapter 15

> "Without the 'or what' portion of the guarantee, it sounds weak and diluted."
-- Chapter 15

> "Reversing risk is the number one way to increase the conversion of an offer. Experienced marketers spend as much time crafting their guarantees as the deliverables themselves."
-- Chapter 15

> "Don't be emotional, just do the math."
-- Chapter 15

> "Guarantees are enhancers. They can enhance the magnetism or attraction of any offer, but they cannot make a business."
-- Chapter 15

> "Best case you get the body of your dreams and we give you all your money towards staying with us to hit your long-term goal. Worst case you tell me I suck, I write you a check, and you get six weeks of free training. Both options are risk free. But, the only thing guaranteed not to help you is walking out of here today."
-- Chapter 15

> "Since it is so standard to have some sort of guarantee, not having one is attention-worthy."
-- Chapter 15

## On Naming

> "Like the tree that falls in the forest that no one hears, having a Grand Slam Offer will not make you money if no one finds out about it."
-- Chapter 16

> "Half-ass naming your product or offering can ruin conversions."
-- Chapter 16

> "Good rhymes stick in people's minds. Rhyme your program name to win the game."
-- Chapter 16

> "Over time, offers fatigue. And in local markets, they fatigue even faster."
-- Chapter 16

> "Once you've monetized an offer, rarely should you change it."
-- Chapter 16
